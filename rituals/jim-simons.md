# 🏛️ Jim Simons' Trading Principles

> "The best models are the ones that are not too complex and capture the right kinds of structure." — Jim Simons

Jim Simons, the mathematician who founded Renaissance Technologies, built the most successful hedge fund in history by treating the stock market as a massive code-breaking problem rather than a financial one. His **Medallion Fund** returned 66% annualised gross from 1988–2018 — an unmatched record that conventional finance cannot explain.

---

## The Five Core Principles

### 1. Don't Ask Why — Ask How

Simons avoided macroeconomic theories, earnings narratives, and news stories entirely. His edge was purely statistical. If the data showed that a pattern repeated with 51% probability, that was a signal — even if no logical explanation existed.

**Practical application for the Sniper:** When your system gives a setup, don't second-guess it with a "gut feeling" about the Fed or some CEO's tweet. The signal is the signal. Execute.

> "We're not economists. We look for patterns. Period."

### 2. The 50.75% Edge

You do not need to be right all the time. Simons' edge was often razor-thin — a fraction of a percent above random. The compounding effect of executing that edge thousands of times with **perfect discipline** is what created billions.

**The Sniper's interpretation:** A 55% win rate with a 1.5:1 reward-to-risk ratio is a highly profitable system over 100 trades. The enemy is not losing trades — it is abandoning the system during drawdowns.

| Win Rate | R:R Ratio | Expected Value per Trade |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 50.75%   | 1.0:1     | +$0.015 per $1 risked    |
| 55%      | 1.5:1     | +$0.275 per $1 risked    |
| 60%      | 2.0:1     | +$0.60 per $1 risked     |

### 3. Systematisation — No Human Override

The single greatest risk to any quantitative system is the human operator's "gut feeling." Once a model is built, backtested, and live-traded, the Medallion Fund's rules were absolute. No analyst could override the model, especially during high-volatility periods when the temptation to intervene was highest.

**The Sniper's rule:** You are allowed to adjust your **system** during your Sunday review session. You are never allowed to override your system **in the moment** during market hours. An in-the-moment override is always a psychological event, never a rational one.

### 4. Data Purity Above All

Renaissance spent more engineering effort cleaning historical data — adjusting for stock splits, dividend payments, delistings, and data errors — than on the algorithms themselves. A signal derived from dirty data is worse than no signal at all.

**Applied to technical analysis:** Before trusting any indicator on your chart, verify that your data source adjusts for splits and dividends. A chart that looks like a breakout might be a split artifact.

### 5. Cross-Disciplinary Intelligence

Simons hired **physicists, mathematicians, and cryptographers** — not finance MBAs. The reason: finance professionals carry cognitive biases about how markets "should" work. Scientists ask only: does this pattern exist, and can we measure it?

**Your edge:** Combining quantitative signals (RSI, OBV, Bollinger Bands) with the Spartan Decision-Making Framework gives you a cross-disciplinary advantage over the emotional retail trader who only reads earnings reports.

## The Medallion Fund's Hidden Rules

1. **No position is held longer than its signal supports.** Time in market without a supporting signal is called "hope trading."
2. **Drawdowns are expected and pre-modelled.** A system that has never had a drawdown has never been stress-tested.
3. **Correlation is the hidden killer.** Two positions that look independent often move together in a crisis. True diversification requires measuring correlation, not just sector labels.
4. **Execution quality matters as much as signal quality.** Slippage, timing, and order size can turn a profitable signal into a losing one.

## Daily Simons Check

Before placing any trade, ask these three questions:

- [ ] Is this trade generated by my system, or by my emotions?
- [ ] Do I know my exact exit level before I enter?
- [ ] Does this trade fit within my Kelly Criterion position size?

If any answer is "no" — **do not enter the trade.**
