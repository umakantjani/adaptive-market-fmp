# 🏛️ The Spartan Decision-Making Framework

> "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a large portion of it." — Seneca

Derived from Howard Raiffa's *Eight Psychological Traps* and applied to quantitative trading, this framework is your psychological operating system. Markets are not won by intelligence alone — they are won by the trader who eliminates systematic cognitive errors before they compound into capital destruction.

The Spartan enters each session not with optimism or pessimism, but with **process**.

---

## The Eight Traps

---

### Trap 1: The Anchoring Trap

**Definition:** Fixating on the first piece of information you received — typically the price you paid — as the reference point for all subsequent decisions.

**Market example:** You bought SOXL at $54.71. It drops to $48. You refuse to exit because you are "anchored" to your entry price, waiting to "break even" instead of acknowledging that the 200-day SMA has broken, volume is declining, and the trade thesis is invalidated.

**The mechanism:** Your brain treats your purchase price as a psychological floor. Every decision is measured against that number, not against the current technical structure.

**The Ritual:** Every morning, look at your positions as if you own zero shares. Ask yourself: *"If I had this dollar amount in cash right now, would I choose to buy this ticker at today's price and current technicals?"* If the answer is no — your anchor is dragging you down. The Spartan exits.

---

### Trap 2: The Status-Quo Trap

**Definition:** Favouring the current situation because change feels like risk, and risk feels like potential regret.

**Market example:** You hold 400 shares of SOXL and they are working. A superior Sniper setup emerges in TSM or AMD — better squeeze, higher RSI alignment, stronger OBV confirmation. You stay in SOXL simply because you are "already in it" and the idea of a swap introduces friction you don't want to manage.

**The mechanism:** The human brain is loss-averse. Staying in the current position feels safe because the loss is unrealised. Moving requires action, and action introduces the possibility of regret.

**The Ritual:** Every Sunday, review your *Alternative Opportunity Cost*. For each position you hold, identify the single best Sniper setup that currently exists in your watchlist. If the alternative is objectively superior by your system's criteria, the status quo is not safety — it is inertia. The Spartan moves capital to the highest-probability trade.

---

### Trap 3: The Sunk-Cost Trap

**Definition:** Making present decisions to justify past mistakes, rather than evaluating the current situation on its own merits.

**Market example:** You sold a covered call, and the underlying ripped 12% above your strike. Rather than buying back the call at a manageable loss and repositioning, you "revenge trade" — adding shares, doubling down on the original thesis — just to prove you were right. The original error is no longer the problem. The compounding response to it is.

**The mechanism:** The money already spent (the "sunk cost") is irretrievable. Continuing to act on it is not strategy — it is ego protection disguised as conviction.

**The Ritual:** Treat every dollar currently in your account as **New Money**. This capital has no memory of what you paid for anything. The market has no idea — and no obligation to care — about what you lost yesterday. The Spartan's internal mantra: *"When you find yourself in a hole, stop digging."*

---

### Trap 4: The Confirming-Evidence Trap

**Definition:** Actively seeking information that confirms your existing position while unconsciously filtering out information that contradicts it.

**Market example:** You are long SOXL. You spend your research time reading AI Revolution articles and bullish chip demand forecasts. You skip the FOMC minutes, the yield curve inversion analysis, and the reports about softening enterprise tech spending. Your information diet is curated to feel good, not to be accurate.

**The mechanism:** Cognitive dissonance is uncomfortable. The brain resolves it by selectively attending to confirming evidence. This is not laziness — it is a hardwired survival mechanism that becomes lethal in markets.

**The Ritual:** Practice **Red Teaming**. For every bullish reason you have to hold or enter a position, you must identify and genuinely engage with one credible bearish argument. If you cannot articulate the bear case more forcefully than a sceptic could, you do not fully understand the trade. The Spartan knows the enemy's argument better than the enemy does.

---

### Trap 5: The Framing Trap

**Definition:** The way you describe a situation to yourself determines what actions feel available, appropriate, or necessary.

**Market example:**

- **Frame A:** "I am losing $500 on this trade." → Triggers fear, impulse to hold and hope, or panic selling.
- **Frame B:** "I am paying an $500 insurance premium to protect the remaining $21,500 of my account." → Triggers disciplined risk management and calm execution of the pre-defined exit.

Same position, same loss, radically different response — because the frame changed.

**The mechanism:** Language shapes perception. Losses framed as "losing" activate the brain's threat-response system. Losses framed as "cost of doing business" activate the rational prefrontal cortex.

**The Ritual:** Reframe all losses as **Insurance Premiums** paid to avoid catastrophic capital destruction. Reframe all profits as **Alpha Harvested** — extracted from inefficient market participants — for your long-term wealth compounding. The Spartan narrates the trade, not reacts to it.

---

### Trap 6: The Overconfidence Trap

**Definition:** Overestimating your ability to predict specific outcomes, especially after a run of recent winners.

**Market example:** After three consecutive winning weeks, you "know" SOXL is going to $70. You stop setting stop-losses because "this one is different." You double your position size beyond what Kelly Criterion permits. You treat conviction as certainty — a distinction that separates the professional from the gambler.

**The mechanism:** Recent success creates a feedback loop. The brain interprets winning streaks as evidence of skill rather than statistical variance. Confidence becomes overconfidence; overconfidence becomes recklessness.

**The Ritual:** Use the **Pre-Mortem** technique before every trade. Assume the trade has already failed — it is three weeks from now and you are writing a post-mortem analysis. Ask: *"What killed this trade?"* List every plausible failure mode: macro reversal, earnings miss, sector rotation, technical breakdown. This grounds your conviction in reality and forces scenario-planning rather than outcome-projection.

---

### Trap 7: The Prudence Trap

**Definition:** Being so cautious that you consistently miss the high-probability entries your system identifies — waiting for a certainty that markets never offer.

**Market example:** A textbook Bollinger Band Squeeze is forming on SOXL. Your Sniper criteria are met: RSI at 58 (green zone), OBV rising, price at the 21-day EMA. But you hesitate because of a 1% pullback from the intraday high. You wait for "more confirmation." By the time you feel comfortable, the stock is up 10% and the entry is gone.

**The mechanism:** The fear of a small, near-term loss outweighs the rational expectation of a larger, medium-term gain. This asymmetry in loss-aversion causes chronic under-trading of valid setups.

**The Ritual:** Trust the **Simons 50.75% Rule**: you do not need certainty, you need a quantifiable edge. When your system says the conditions for entry are met, the Spartan enters — without modification, without delay. Perfect is the enemy of executed. The cost of missing a 10% move waiting for certainty is permanent and irretrievable. The cost of a 2% stop-loss is planned and finite.

---

### Trap 8: The Recallability Trap

**Definition:** Giving disproportionate weight to recent, dramatic, or emotionally vivid events when assessing current probabilities.

**Market example:** SOXL dropped 5% last Friday in a flash of heavy selling. It is now Monday. Your system is generating a valid sell-call setup based on RSI, Bollinger structure, and OBV. But you are frozen — the Friday drop is vivid in your memory, and you interpret today's setup through the lens of Friday's fear.

**The mechanism:** The brain recalls recent and dramatic events more easily than statistical averages. This availability bias distorts your probability assessment — recent events feel more probable than historical base rates justify.

**The Ritual:** Look at **Long-Term Averages**. One Red Friday is noise. The 200-day SMA is signal. Go to the weekly chart. Zoom out. Ask: *"In the context of the last 52 weeks, what does this chart structure indicate?"* The Spartan separates the drama of yesterday from the data of the present.

## Your Daily Anti-Trap Checklist

**Perform at 9:15 AM ET — before the market opens.**

- [ ] **Anchor Check:** Am I holding any position because I like the stock, or because I am anchored to my buy price?
- [ ] **Status-Quo Check:** Is there a better trade than what I currently hold? Have I genuinely evaluated alternatives?
- [ ] **Sunk-Cost Check:** Am I in any position to "prove I was right" rather than because the thesis is intact?
- [ ] **Confirming-Evidence Check:** Have I read one credible bearish argument this morning?
- [ ] **Framing Check:** Am I narrating today's session as "winning and losing" or as "following my process"?
- [ ] **Overconfidence Check:** Have I run a Pre-Mortem on any new trade I plan to enter today?
- [ ] **Prudence Check:** Am I avoiding any valid system setup out of vague unease rather than concrete system criteria?
- [ ] **Recall Check:** Am I weighting recent dramatic events more than long-term statistical averages?

## The Spartan Oath

The Spartan trader does not trade markets. The Spartan trades a **process** — and the process is built to be right over 1,000 repetitions, not on any single day.

Markets will be irrational. Your job is not to predict irrationality — it is to remain rational within it.

*Discipline equals freedom.*
